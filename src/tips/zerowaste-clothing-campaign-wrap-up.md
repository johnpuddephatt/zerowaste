During COP26, we decided to launch #ZeroWasteClothing to explore the world of clothing and its environmental impact. Throughout the two weeks of the campaign we have been asking ourselves- what can we do as individuals to help slow down fast fashion? 

We wanted to explore the different factors that make clothing a significant contributor of GHG emissions and understand practical ways we can help reduce its impact. It has been an exciting campaign and one that has sparked a lot of internal and external discussion and collaboration. Here is a summary of highlights from the campaign.





# THE RESEARCH PROCESS



We started off by doing some research on the environmental impact of clothing and the different factors contributing to the industry footprint. This pie chart that helped us set the scene for our campaign. It is based on a  [recent report](https://www.policyconnect.org.uk/research/net-zero-exchanges-connecting-policy-and-research-climate-action) by Leeds University academic, Dr Mark Sumner, a research paper that has guided our thinking throughout our campaign. 

![](/uploads/zerowasteleeds_252413982_242697594507280_3602072541199574406_n.jpg)

One week into our campaign, we were also delighted to host Dr. Sumner for one our Facebook Lunchtime Lives to discuss his research in more detail and what we can do as individuals to help reduce the impact of clothing on the environment.

# THE CAMPAIGN

![](/uploads/untitled-design-22-.png)

We structured our campaign around 4 main call to action- take better care of your clothes, buy better, wear clothes for longer and use your voice and influence.



# TAKE BETTER CARE OF YOUR CLOTHES



The first theme we decided to explore was garment care. During our research, we were surprised to find out that 20% of all emissions relating to clothing result from the way we wash, dry and iron our clothes.

 That’s why a key aim of #ZeroWasteClothing was to encourage people to rethink the ways they take care of their clothes and to provide practical tips on how to switch to more sustainable habits including washing less and at low temperatures, checking garment labels and choosing to air dry clothes.

# WEAR CLOTHES FOR LONGER 



If we all extended the use of our clothes by just nine months, therefore delaying the purchase of new clothes, we could reduce the carbon footprint by 8%!

That’s why wearing your clothes for longer has been a key theme of our campaign. There are several ways to extend the life of our clothes and make sure we wear them for a longer period of time- from buying clothes we can wear more often to repairing the ones we own and donating the items we no longer need.
